Is the ISTQB-AI Certification Still Relevant in 2026?
When discussing testing and AI, two ISTQB certifications are regularly mentioned... and often confused, despite having very different scopes. I took the Certified Tester AI Testing (CT-AI) exam, and I highly recommend it to anyone involved in testing software that incorporates AI.
Active in software testing for nearly 10 years, I faced a major challenge for the first time at the end of 2024: testing a chatbot. An infinity of possibilities, unpredictable or barely predictable results, excessive expectations, and even new roles in the team: I had to rethink all my certainties.
CT-AI or CT-GenAI?
This is probably the most asked question about testing and AI since the release of the new ISTQB Tester with Generative AI certification in July 2025. However, the content of this latest certification is very different from its predecessor, AI Testing, released in 2021 and translated into French in 2022.
As their respective titles indicate, CT-AI is aimed at those who test AI-based products, while CT-GenAI will help any tester integrate AI into their processes, whether the system under test is traditional and deterministic or incorporates an AI model or service.
For me, both are relevant! But when I started exploring this new field, CT-GenAI didn't exist yet. That will be for 2026!
Does CT-AI from 2021... the Prehistory of AI?
Most analysts agree that by the end of the decade, the majority of software products will incorporate AI. Let's remember that AI isn't just large language models: data analysis, prediction, recommendations, computer vision, disease detection, and even cybersecurity—AI is everywhere. For a software tester, knowing how to tackle these new challenges is at least as important as being able to optimize their work thanks to these new tools.
But CT-AI dates from 2021, year 1 before ChatGPT, and therefore addresses these famous LLMs that are revolutionizing IT and testing only marginally. Does it remain relevant?
In a word: yes! Whether or not AI incorporates language processing, the challenges remain comparable: non-deterministic behavior, data management, robustness, security and ethical considerations, not to mention the human dimension. Indeed, this rapidly changing field generates as many fantasies as sometimes excessive promises.
What I Learned
We'll start unsurprisingly with a definition of what AI covers... in 2021, as well as an overview of related technologies, frameworks and hardware components used, and associated standards. Perhaps not the most enduring part of the syllabus—the authors are actually cautious, regularly reminding us that the content describes the situation "in April 2021"—but it demonstrates the extent of the field... and the vocabulary that will need to be mastered to communicate with Data Scientists (special mention for the term "regression," which has a completely different meaning in AI).
Much more interesting is the second chapter, devoted to the quality characteristics of AI-based systems. Beyond demonstrating that the system works, the tester will have to determine how it evolves and adapts to its environment. They will also have to question the risks of bias and the ethical dimension, and know how to explain the key concepts of Explainable AI (XAI): transparency, interpretability, and explicability.
You'll then need to understand the basics of Machine Learning and neural networks to grasp their risks and validation methods. This part is very technical, especially for those without experience in AI development, and actually required much more effort from me than the rest. But beyond intellectual satisfaction, it's an essential step to understand the work of AI developers and facilitate communication with them. Even if your project is content to integrate existing AI components, it remains crucial to grasp their capabilities and limitations. In particular, the chapter on functional performance metrics of ML allows for precise description of probabilistic behavior, notably by distinguishing the concepts of accuracy, precision, and recall, as well as other relevant AI-specific indicators. Although ML-oriented, many of these concepts are applicable to other branches of AI. Another long chapter describes the importance of data processing (the syllabus cites the figure of 43% of ML workflow effort), a field that will certainly interest testers faced with datasets of a size incomparable to what they handle in traditional development.
Chapters 7 to 9 get to the heart of the matter, describing the validation of AI-based systems, the evaluation of new quality characteristics, and the methods and techniques employed. Although some exercises are offered, don't expect a ready-to-deploy manual, but rather a list of issues and recommendations. This is actually often the case with ISTQB certifications and it's what makes them resistant to technical evolution: even if the means change, the dangers persist...
Among other aspects, numerous passages mention the importance of questioning the relationship between humans and machines, a question that has taken on a whole new dimension since the development of large language models.
The journey ends with a short chapter dedicated to test environments, then a final part devoted to the use of AI in this field. This is probably the section that has evolved the most since the syllabus was published, and even though many points remain relevant, the new CT-GenAI certification is undoubtedly more suitable for covering this aspect.
How to Approach It?
I studied and took the certification independently, combining numerous sources. So I can't comment on the available training courses, but given the scope of the material, it's probably the most efficient approach.
For my part, I supplemented the syllabus with various resources to compensate for my lack of experience in AI. Among these, I would mention:
Elements of AI: two free online courses offered by the University of Helsinki. The first part (Introduction to AI) has been carefully translated into many languages, including French. The second part "Building AI," equally interesting, is more practical and requires basic knowledge of Python programming (although alternative exercises are always offered to less technical audiences). These courses are very educational and constitute an excellent starting point before diving into the ISTQB syllabus.
Google for Developers offers dozens of online training courses in the field of AI and many others, including a suite devoted to Machine Learning. Whether to better grasp the concepts mentioned or to go further, it's an ideal companion and probably more up-to-date in certain areas.
I used many other sites and online resources, the subject obviously being widely developed. I was also fortunate to be immersed in a talented team of Data Scientists who were able to answer my questions and challenge my new knowledge... but above all to directly apply these concepts through two concrete projects.
Finally, I admit without shame: ChatGPT greatly helped me review. The learning mode is impressive and will allow you to review the entire material before taking the exam.
3 Concrete Transformations of My Job
As I said in the introduction, testing software that exploits AI required a new definition of my work, mainly in these three aspects:
Requirements Engineering, Communication with Stakeholders, and User Testing
A traditional, deterministic system can be (almost) exhaustively described in the form of functional and non-functional requirements. This is also necessary for an AI-based system, but it requires a completely different approach to define measurable and achievable results. It's rarely possible to achieve a 100% success rate: functional requirements will have to be based on statistical approaches and thresholds to be reached. It's also necessary to look at new quality attributes that are sometimes more important than functional performance: XAI, absence of bias, ethics...
This is particularly true for language-based developments like a chatbot: it's about measuring the added value for a user based on subjective elements... that we'll need to objectify.
Finally, a chatbot seems to have no limits. It's necessary to guide stakeholders in defining a realistic project, to raise awareness among everyone about the associated risks, and to remain ready for change in a constantly evolving field.
Work Within the Implementation Team
Those who know me know how much I'm a proponent of total integration between development and testing, as promoted for example by holistic testing. This is even more crucial in the field of AI. Due to its exploratory nature and the new associated risks, the time devoted to validation is generally greater than in traditional development—some sources mention 50 to 60% of total time, but this obviously varies greatly depending on the type of project.
Serious data scientists develop their own procedures, which the tester will have to understand and sometimes complement. The role of hub between stakeholders, users, and developers is reinforced, and it's often necessary to delve deeper into the field to grasp the issues and identify relevant metrics. Finally, in a complex system, it remains essential to validate each component, whether or not it incorporates AI, to avoid the "black box" effect that makes exploiting results impossible. A global strategy integrating AI and traditional development will be one of the keys to success.
Statistical Testing and Language Processing
The best way to validate a non-deterministic system is to repeat each case many times, often by varying the input data, to determine scores or metrics on a statistical basis. This inevitably involves automation. In the case of a chatbot or any other application incorporating language processing, it will also be necessary to use new methods such as LLM-as-a-judge... which themselves exploit AI. The snake bites its tail, but humans remain at the heart of the process to maintain control.
The new techniques based on the use of LLMs are not described in this syllabus, they didn't exist yet in 2021. But the points of attention and the way of organizing things presented remain an extremely relevant guide.
In summary, active technological watch combined with rigorous analysis as described within this certification allowed me to develop a methodology adapted to the challenges, but also to implement it and defend it to the team and decision-makers. This is also the added value of ISTQB certifications: being able to inscribe your project in coherence with the industry, describe it rigorously, and defend its relevance with recognized arguments.
What About GenAI Then?
The ISTQB CT-GenAI certification trains testers to use generative AI in software validation processes, whether or not that software incorporates AI. It therefore in no way replaces CT-AI (a rumor even mentions a new edition for the latter, although no draft is currently available). If you're looking to optimize your processes and understand the challenge of generative AI in the field, it's probably a better investment. If you need to test AI or hybrid systems, both are complementary...
